Ishtar was like an Uber driver at the airport: you never knew what move she would make next.

Huzalu knew exactly what that felt like, given he was currently being driven by an Uber driver at the airport. The driver’s name was Muranu and he had a four-point-nine star average with one and a half thousand ratings to his name. Somewhere in Huzalu’s prefrontal cortex, he knew Muranu was probably a better driver than he’d ever be, but apparently his amygdala wasn’t getting the message.

<i> “As I recall, I know you love to show off <br>
But I never thought that you would take it this far.” </i>

The radio sounded like Kanye, but he didn’t know the song. Ishtar probably would; he’d ask her later.

Forty-five minutes later, Huzalu realized he had no chance of getting a question in. He’d made it to the city, Ishtar’s hometown, and she was so, so excited to see him. She asked how he was, and what he’d been up to, and if he’d met any cute girls in the months since they’d last seen each other. He told her he had—in fact, he had a girlfriend. She asked why he hadn’t posted their photos on Instagram; he said he’d stopped using it, so she asked for the photos he would have posted, and he begrudgingly scrolled through his Photos app with Ishtar looking over his shoulder. She took the opportunity to talk about all the girls she’d seen over the months—Huzalu counted at least five—which, naturally, went until Ishtar decided it was time for dinner. 

They ended up at a casual Mediterranean place down the street. Huzalu managed to bring up the Kanye song there. It was Flashing Lights, one of Ishtar’s favorites, along with Champion and I Wonder. She spent a few minutes analyzing each as Huzalu nodded along. He told her she’d enjoy Driftwood by The People Versus, as well as a couple other indie artists he’d been listening to recently. She slotted each one into its appropriate playlist. 

He commented that he’d be listening to Driftwood tonight at his hotel. Ishtar said she thought he’d be staying with her. He told her that she never offered, but it was fine, the hotel wasn’t all that expensive anyway. That was okay, of course, but Ishtar said she’d invited a couple of local friends over who had heard so much about Huzalu and wanted to meet him. And she had a bottle of St. Germain. 

Huzalu could never turn down an elderflower spritz.

They arrived at her apartment at 8:30. Huzalu just had time to update a friend at home before Ishtar’s friends arrived with a speaker and a bottle of white wine. One took Huzalu into the kitchen to pour him a spritz with a generous amount of wine on top. They chatted for ten minutes or so about nothing in particular—the governor’s race, his hometown, Kanye again.

<i>“Is the good life better than the life I live?”</i>

They taught Huzalu how to play mahjong. He won the first game, which required a roar of empty excitement and another celebratory glass of wine. 

After the fourth, he said he needed to go to the bathroom. It was a familiar feeling. One drink was noticeable—he was a lightweight—but only in the muted quiet of the bathroom, where he had a chance to look at his reflection and feel the strange weight of his mind when there was nothing going on inside. Alcohol tended to open two holes in his brain: one at the top and one at the bottom, so thoughts could never stay more than an instant. Behind the door, his head was an empty husk, with only a trickle of Ishtar’s music passing through.

<i> “I’m trippin’, this drink got me saying a lot <br>
But I know that God put you in front of me <br>
So how the hell could you front on me? <br>
There’s a thousand you’s, there’s only one of me.”</i>

He checked his watch. 10:30. Probably time for him to head to his hotel.

He re-emerged and slowly fought his way out, waiting for the right break in the conversation. Ishtar gave him a halfhearted goodbye and a side hug. 

The hotel was just over a mile away. He walked, earbuds in, music loud but not deafening, as usual.

<i>“This is the story of a champion <br>
Runners on their mark and they pop the guns <br>
Stand up, stand up, here [s]he comes <br>
Tell me what it takes to be number one <br>
Tell me what it takes to be number one.”</i>
