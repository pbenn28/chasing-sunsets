On March 28, 1979, and for several days after, Three Mile Island in Pennsylvania was the site of the United States’ worst commercial nuclear accident. The core was damaged, radiation was released, and thousands of residents evacuated the area, according to a memorial at the site (Pell, 2019). Then, in 2024, Microsoft announced that the Three Mile Island reactor would reopen to power a new AI data center, newly branded as the Crane Clean Energy Center (Crownhart, 2024). 

To a generation that grew up in the shadow of the disaster, that statement might come as a shock, but the move is part of a string of new nuclear construction projects driven by AI energy usage. Alongside the Three Mile Island deal, Amazon and other software companies have struck similar agreements to commission their own nuclear power sites. AI companies turn to nuclear power because it provides 24/7 power that can operate for years at a time without refueling, with stable costs that are fairly low during operation (Office of Nuclear Energy, 2025). It also ensures access to a stable energy source disconnected from market prices. 

However, these nuclear deals do not provide nearly enough power. Data centers have caused energy shortages across the country, including in Pennsylvania, where a 2025 capacity auction fell short of its intended reserve capacity after hitting a state-mandated cap on maximum electricity costs (Hall, 2025). The U.S. Department of Energy found that data center energy usage is increasing rapidly and could consume as much as 12 percent of U.S. energy by 2028 (Energy.gov, 2024). Going further, former OpenAI researcher Leopold Aschenbrenner predicts that data center energy demand alone could exceed the entire current U.S. energy supply by 2030 (2024). Spiking energy demand is bound to spike energy prices for those who do not own their power sources, including most consumers.

In that case, one might expect AI companies to build their own energy supply, which would be relatively cheaper than market-rate electricity and ensure enough electricity is always available. Unfortunately, building new nuclear power capacity is extraordinarily expensive. In the 1960s, average reactor construction cost was as low as $1500 per kilowatt in 2026 dollars. Some modern reactors cost as much as ten times that amount; one Georgia plant is expected to require $8000 per kilowatt in immediate costs, with actual costs being nearly double that amount due to financing costs like interest on loans (Potter, 2023). For reference, solar power generally costs less than $3000 per kilowatt to install (Walker, 2026). Although the day-to-day costs of running nuclear reactors are relatively small, the enormous investment required for construction makes them poor options for new energy capacity. Notably, this problem is not a global one; South Korea has significantly developed its nuclear capacity in part because new reactors come in closer to $2000 per kilowatt (Potter, 2023). 

Authors generally attribute the extraordinary cost of U.S. reactors to increases in regulation during the late 1960s and 1970s. Paik and Schriver (1980) found that reactor construction costs increased 143% on average between 1971 and 1977, primarily driven by “new regulatory requirements for additional safety and environmental features” that required longer and more detailed design and construction (p. 1). This is no surprise—Komanoff (1981) found that the number of Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) and Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) "regulatory guides" expanded from 21 in 1971 to 143 in 1978 (p. 24), with a concurrent 5-fold increase in the number of questions asked during licensing reviews (Szalay, 1978, p. 15). Potter (2023) finds that these regulations generally do not directly impact safety; rather than requiring materials to hold up to higher standards, they often only require extensive documentation and testing, causing a significant increase in labor costs. Potter also finds that the unpredictability of regulation often requires design changes that stall the project and add new costs.

This paper explores why and how extensive nuclear energy regulations were put in place. The impetus for new regulation comes from the social amplification of risk, a model that describes how risk perception propagates through a population and worsens fears of poorly understood risks like nuclear accidents. These fears are translated into new laws and regulations when political opportunities are available, allowing popular movements and opinions to shape government actions. Finally, these policies remain in place for long periods of time, an equilibrium only punctuated by moments when fear overcomes institutional gridlock and government motivation. Understanding how governments arrive at well-informed nuclear energy policies informs recommendations for government actions that will result in the same kind of policy in the future. 

### HOW NUCLEAR POWER CAME TO BE

Atomic energy was born from the atomic bomb. In 1940, the so-called Frisch-Peierls Memorandum conceived of the ability to “[utilize] the energy stored in atomic nuclei as a source of energy” (Frisch & Peierls, 1940). In 1945, the Atomic Energy Commission was created with the goal of harnessing this energy, and it was soon successful. The first reactors came online in 1954 in the U.S. and U.S.S.R., 1956 in the U.K., and 1959 in France (Intl. Atomic Energy Agency, 2004). In the 1960s, utility companies began taking responsibility for building reactors, resulting in competitive bidding wars between different companies with different reactor designs (Le Renard, 2017, p. 59). Reactors were cheap and exciting, with dreams of electricity that was “too cheap to meter” and predictions that the U.S. alone would have over a thousand individual reactors (Intl. Atomic Energy Agency, 2004). 

That dream of limitless energy was soon abandoned. In a 1997 PBS documentary, a focus group was asked what came to mind when they thought of nuclear energy. They said “disaster,” “trouble,” and “radiation” (Palfreman). The public imagination of nuclear power today is dominated by images of accidents: of Three Mile Island, where a stuck-open valve compounded into a core meltdown; of Chernobyl, the explosion that exposed millions across Europe to radiation; and of Fukushima, where an earthquake and tsunami caused a series of electrical malfunctions that resulted in an explosion. Nuclear reactor construction has waxed and waned over decades, but it has never come close to reaching the levels seen before Three Mile Island and Chernobyl.

### MISTRUST AND RESISTANCE

In places where it occurred, the slowdown of nuclear power construction was primarily a result of popular resistance, both in the form of protest and organizations attempting to influence policy. In other terms, the nuclear slowdown was a bottom-up process rather than a top-down one originating from policymakers’ opinions. In the U.S., the flurry of new regulations issued by the Atomic Energy Commission (AEC) during the 1970s intended to “reassure the public, and undercut the appeal of its critics,” in response to a growing sense that “public distrust of the AEC was growing and support for nuclear power was declining” (Walker & Wellock, 2010, p. 43, 45). Debate over these requirements spilled into the media and public sphere, resulting in highly publicized protests at proposed new plants, including a 1977 demonstration at the Seabrook plant in New Hampshire that was an "embarrassment" for the state’s nuclear power proponents (Katz & List, 1981, p. 59). Parallel demonstrations played out across Europe, including at a 1975 demonstration in Wyhl, Germany in which protestors were “dragged through the mud” by police, “[capturing] national attention and [sparking]... further demonstrations all over the country” (Moraal & Shan, 2023). 

Protests like these continued to a lesser extent following the 1970s. A short burst of protests erupted after the accident at Chernobyl, including demonstrations with over 200,000 participants across Italy, prompting a 1987 referendum that shut down the country’s nuclear energy program entirely (Giugni, 2004). However, since the vast majority of countries had already reduced their nuclear energy ambitions by the late 1980s, active demonstrations were largely not needed. This would soon change, as the 2000s saw a revival of many nuclear energy programs that prompted a new wave of protests, including a 2010 demonstration in Germany in which protestors formed a 75-mile human chain (Reuters, 2010). These demonstrations exploded worldwide following the 2011 accident at Fukushima.

Authors often incorrectly claim that changes in nuclear energy policy are triggered by major events like accidents. In the French context, authors such as Godefroy (2020) identify the 1973 oil crisis as the cause of the 1974 Messmer Plan, the government plan responsible for France’s extensive nuclear energy system. However, Le Renard (2017) notes that the plan was part of a broader French effort to implement nuclear power, and that it was only possible because France already “[had] considerable experience in [the] area” (p. 53). In the parallel U.S. context, Walker & Wellock (2010) argue that opposition to nuclear energy was a response to fears triggered by Three Mile Island and Chernobyl. The authors present the Seabrook demonstration as an example of this result, omitting that its most fervent opposition took place in 1977 before both accidents. A report from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2023) claims that opposition to new nuclear reactor builds increased after the three major accidents listed below. While this is true, each accident had a small immediate effect on public opinion. Instead, changes in public opinion take several years to propagate, a phenomenon that the authors do not account for. In every case, changes in public opinion occur over long periods of time and without single causes, rather than being immediately triggered by accidents and major events.

In that case, why can movements form without a specific grounded fear, and why can fears worsen long after the immediate threat of an accident? Both of these questions have been extensively studied in the world of sociology. The primary framework for understanding nuclear fears is the so-called social amplification of risk (SARF) framework, proposed by Kasperson et al. (1988). Individual perception of risk is generally biased by simplifications required to understand complex phenomena, alignment with social groups with specific ideologies, and the potential of a risk to signal larger uncontrollable harm (p. 185-186). Based on these heuristics, individuals or groups act as nodes that receive and filter information before possibly re-transmitting it, choosing to either amplify or attenuate the magnitude of the risk. The SARF framework imagines this as a so-called ripple effect, with information spreading outward from a primary source (e.g., a direct witness or scientist), followed by re-transmission by organizations and institutions, traditional media organizations, and other actors on social media (Kasperson, 2022, p. 1368). As the signal passes through many different nodes, it may be progressively amplified or attenuated many times, resulting in a compounding effect with the potential to severely magnify risks.

One of the most extensively studied applications of the SARF framework is the nuclear energy context, with Kasperson’s original paper citing the accident at Three Mile Island as a core example. Nuclear energy is considered an example of risk amplification because, importantly, nuclear power plants are extraordinarily safe. While Three Mile Island was the most severe accident in U.S. history, it cannot properly be called the deadliest, because no deaths have been directly attributed to the accident. No deaths occurred during the accident, and the surrounding communities did not receive significant radiation, with an average dose below 10 millirem—equivalent to less than two weeks of background radiation (Palfreman et al., 1997). Talbott et al. (2000) found increases in cancer rates in surrounding counties, but they also stated that “it is unlikely [to be] related to radiation exposure on the day of the accident.” In Fukushima, a similar spike in thyroid cancer cases was identified, but the U.N. and Japanese government found that it was most likely due to overscreening rather than radiation exposure (Fackler, 2025). As the most astounding of the three major nuclear accidents, Chernobyl has resulted in thousands of deaths, with some disagreement over the exact number; a U.N. report found that under 4,000 had died, while a 2006 study estimated as high as 16,000 (Peplow, 2006). Two factors make this number more palatable. First, an accident like Chernobyl is extremely unlikely to happen again, as safety standards and reactor designs have progressed far beyond the problematic RBMK design used at the Chernobyl plant (Dever, 2022). Second, Vohra et al. (2021) estimate that the burning of fossil fuels is responsible for 10.2 million premature deaths per year worldwide. While nuclear radiation is dangerous, the scale of its impact is miniscule compared to the alternative.

Despite its safety, nuclear energy is associated with high risk because of social amplification. Recall that individual risk perception has three major biases: required simplification, ideological alignment, and potential signals of future harm. First, nuclear energy is inherently complex, and plants’ safety systems are even more difficult to understand, meaning few outsiders understand the intricacies and improvements made to plant designs. Thus, the popular understanding of nuclear energy becomes a simplified one, characterized by long-lasting mental images like those of radiation and uncontrollable reactions. Second, while nuclear energy is less polarized than many issues, it can easily become a matter of politics. This is what happened in the U.S. in 1975, when debates over Gerald Ford’s Energy Independence Act were “transformed into a question of the proper role[] of the state” in contrast to the free market, causing Democrats to oppose the bill’s nuclear power sections (Jasper, 1990, p. 119). Finally, with a technology that outsiders struggle to understand, accidents and public debates over safety signal that it may be less trustworthy than experts claim, resulting in a fear of another accident that could expose individuals to dangerous radiation. These reasons are only made worse by the human tendency of risk aversion, in which most people prefer outcomes with certainty over uncertain outcomes with higher reward (Damodaran, 2015). It is no surprise that many people prefer not to live with what they perceive as a significant and uncertain risk, resulting in movements attempting to eliminate that risk. 

### POLITICAL OPPORTUNITIES: HANDLES ON POWER

While the SARF framework is useful for explaining individual perception, it does not explain how policymakers react to that perception. One framework that effectively explains this connection is that of political opportunity theory, first formulated by Eisinger (1973). Kitschelt (1986) identifies that the successes and failures of early anti-nuclear movements in the U.S. and western Europe were explained by a careful analysis of the so-called political opportunities available within each country. A nation’s political opportunity structure is defined by its “configuration[] of resources, institutional arrangements[,] and historical precedents for social mobilization,” defining the environment movements work with rather than their psychological motivations and internal structure (p. 58). In more practical terms, political opportunities are typically a measure of how “open and responsive” a government is to change (Eisinger, 1973, p. 1). Various authors, including Kitschelt, also include in their frameworks a dimension of government capacity, since strong governments respond very differently to public opinion than weak governments (Meyer & Minkoff, 2004, p. 1460). Importantly, these factors are external to popular movements themselves.

Changes in policy occur rapidly and extensively when there are expansive political opportunities. Recall that U.S. nuclear energy policy came to fruition throughout the 1970s, in part due to the 1971 Calvert Cliffs case, a Supreme Court decision holding the AEC to an expansive interpretation of the National Environmental Protection Act, or NEPA (Walker & Wellock, 2010, p. 44). First, the case mandated that the agency consider environmental data and reduce environmental damage “to the fullest extent possible,” immediately increasing the burden of regulatory approval (Swartz, 1997). NEPA and similar laws also gave environmentalists grounds to sue, slowing down projects and instituting new requirements for the agency. According to Walker & Wellock, public groups took advantage of this because they already distrusted the AEC, primarily due to debates over issues like emergency cooling systems (ECCSs) that amplified fears of an accident. This would force the AEC to hold more hearings to prove they considered public opinion, resulting in months of hearings over ECCSs that produced 22,000 pages of transcripts (p. 47). This snowball effect was the result of political opportunities under NEPA that gave citizens the power to intervene in nuclear projects, forcing the creation of more and more obstructive regulation in the years after 1971.

This form of decentralized regulation is relatively unique to the U.S. and applies less in the European context, where political opportunities focus more on central officials’ positions. In fact, Kitschelt (1986) described Germany as an example of a closed state for exactly this reason—regulatory intervention is rare and political opportunities depend more on “procedural representation” in the legislature through elections (p. 75). But even when opportunities are rare, they can be enormously powerful. Chien (2015) analyzes the responses of six European countries to the accident at Fukushima, finding that only two—Germany and Switzerland—implemented sweeping changes to their nuclear energy system (p. 392). Germany’s response was uniquely decisive, with former Prime Minister Angela Merkel deciding to shut down half of the country’s nuclear power plants within just three days of the accident (Spiegel Politik, 2011a). This coincided with two major events. First, Germany experienced anti-nuclear power demonstrations with more than 250,000 people immediately following the accident (Spiegel Politik, 2011b). Second, Germany had three state elections within the month after the accident, and a nationwide election six months after (Chien, 2015, p. 394). It seems that the German and Swiss governments implemented these changes to try to win public favor, and they did: of the six countries Chien studied, those that held elections suffered “elite damage,” with the ruling party losing a significant fraction of their seats in power (p. 395). Thus, public movements forced officials to make a decision: either conform to their policy demands or face electoral damage. 

To show that the election was the forcing factor in this policy change, Chien notes that Sweden—which held no major elections in the two years after Fukushima—experienced no change in its current nuclear energy outlook. That came despite the country lacking a strong position, having decided on a phase-out multiple times in the 1980s before gradually abandoning the plan in decisions from 1994 to 2010 (p. 396). Fear-based movements are effective when political opportunities are abundant and ineffective when those opportunities do not exist.

### LOCKING THE DOOR TO POLICY CHANGE

While policy change seems to be happening constantly, most individual policy issues are relatively stable. Most issues remain out of sight of policymakers at any given time, remaining dormant in the background before coming to the forefront of political debates for arbitrary reasons, including major events like the accident at Fukushima. This is the basis of a political theory known as punctuated equilibrium theory, which presents policy change as a “disjoint and abrupt process… with long periods of stability separating the shifts” (Jones & Baumgartner, 2012, p. 3). The theory presents a model of policy dominated by negative feedback, which maintains stability and reverts small changes back to their original state. At times, however, “change begets change” in a positive feedback loop, resulting in major policy shifts (p. 3). This model stands in contrast to incrementalism, the theory that small policy changes made consistently result in major shifts over long periods of time. However, the two are not necessarily contradictory. It may be true that incremental change occurs in periods between major shifts, a theory that Prindle (2012) describes as “punctuated incrementalism.” In any case, it seems true that there exist both long periods with relatively little policy change and short periods with significant change. 

This theory maps very easily onto the theory of political opportunities. Popular movements are perennially present, which means that their ineffectiveness during these periods may be explained by a lack of political opportunities. In contrast, a period of significant change may be characterized by the presence of one or multiple political opportunities that are taken advantage of in a positive feedback system. However, the mechanism by which opportunities open and close is not obvious.

Equilibrium periods with few political opportunities can be separated into at least two kinds of negative feedback. The first is a form of negative feedback created by a government, typically one with a strong state capacity, in which it intentionally ignores popular movements in favor of internal policy decisions. This is most clearly illustrated in the French case, for which we will explore one example. Recall that French regulators in the 1970s dismissed anti-nuclear protestors as “irrational as well as ignorant” and continued their long-term expansion of nuclear power (Jasper, 1990, p. 148). This is possible because the French government is fundamentally a closed system, a term Kitschelt (1986) applies because it has a “clearly dominant” executive branch and parties that coalesce into blocs rather than accommodating new actors (p. 65). That executive branch entrusted “just one American reactor model and… one agency, EDF, to run the system,” creating an even more centralized and standardized nuclear program (Palfreman et al., 1997). It was also advised by the PEON Commission, an advisory commission created in 1955 from executives in French energy agencies that conducted reviews of safety and reactor feasibility (Le Renard, 2017, p. 57). This level of centralization meant the executive branch was always well-informed with trusted policy recommendations, allowing them to confidently shrug off protests.

In contrast, a more dangerous form of negative feedback exists in which political opportunities are shut down for reasons other than a well-informed decision to implement the best policy. One surprising example of this is in post-Fukushima Japan. Although local support for nuclear energy dropped enormously following the accident, with nearly two-thirds of the Japanese population supporting a reduction in its use, “Japan's energy policy has stayed remarkably true to its [pre-2011] course” (Fraser & Aldrich, 2020, p. 8). Immediately after the accident, the Japanese government shut down the country’s reactors, requiring strict standards for reactors to restart. The government also sought to “revamp the set of regulators,” pushing out entrenched interests like utility companies in favor of new actors (p. 3). However, this failed for several reasons. The so-called nuclear village of utility companies remained at a local level, and local officials soon pushed for restarts to “preserve their extensive compensation packages” from the government as well as the benefits of local jobs and industry (Aldrich & Fraser, 2017, p. 8). When concerned citizens tried to challenge the government’s decision to implement restarts, the Ministry of Justice reassigned the judge to a different city, removing any opportunity for sympathy (Fraser & Aldrich, 2020, p. 4). Rather than making an informed decision, the Japanese equilibrium came from a system of perverse incentives and regulatory capture that creates the potential for misguided policy.

A similarly dangerous equilibrium occurred in post-Fukushima Germany and Taiwan, where partisan ideological coherence prevented officials from changing their policy course after fears from Fukushima subsided. In Germany, after Prime Minister Merkel’s decision for a slow nuclear phase-out (Atomausstieg) in early 2011, the party continued to support the decision as fears from Fukushima gradually subsided. While Merkel could claim in 2012 that there was a “consensus across German society on phasing out certain forms of energy, like nuclear power,” that consensus quickly evaporated. By 2022, only 15% of Germans supported closing nuclear power plants, with 41% supporting a delayed closure and another 41% preferring their long-term use (van Halm, 2022). Despite the shift, Merkel stood by her platform, acknowledging that it was “ambitious and challenging” but refusing to reverse course (Rinke, 2021). In Taiwan, a similar process transpired. In the 2012 and 2016 elections, both parties adopted stances directed towards a “nuclear-free homeland,” inspired by similar transitions worldwide (Ho, 2021, p. 2). However, fears from Fukushima had quickly faded—in part due to highly successful pro-nuclear campaigning—such that in a 2018 referendum, nearly 60% of voters recommended repealing the deadline for abandoning nuclear energy (p. 2). Polling indicated that attitudes towards nuclear energy were “significantly associated with which political party a person supports” (Hsieh, 2020). As a result, the out-of-power KMT adopted a pro-nuclear stance and began attacking the ruling DPP, who could no longer change their decision without seeming to give in to the opposition.

The story of nuclear energy is one dominated by moments of drastic policy change, often during moments of major protest or immediately after accidents. These moments are followed by decades in which policy becomes locked into place, either because the government still believes in the current course or because perverse incentives prevent the government from changing course. Officials looking to implement beneficial policies might rejoice that their efforts may stand the test of time, but their policies will be harder to implement as a result. 

### THE FUTURE OF ENERGY

Faced with rapidly increasing energy demand from AI companies, policymakers should relax regulations on nuclear energy to allow new development and alleviate pressure on market energy prices. Given the model of a punctuated equilibrium, these policies would be likely to stand the test of time without another major issue to prompt new regulation. In order to not leave us stuck with ineffective laws and regulations, these policies should be particularly well-informed and effectively prioritize long-term safety over opinions about the technology that change between years. 

Officials should thus work to address two issues in the development of good policy. First, they should reduce the extent to which momentary movements and socially amplified fears can influence nuclear energy policy. This can be done at several stages in the process of movement development. Officials can slow the positive feedback loop of risk amplification by correcting false information and publicly providing information regarding the safety of nuclear energy. Palfreman et al. (1997) find that one reason nuclear power is trusted in France is because nuclear experts are trusted and offer substantial public information, including tours at local power plants. Other countries can replicate this openness to avoid skepticism of science. On the government side, officials can reduce political opportunities within the regulatory process, allowing them to make regulations that work best for expert organizations and companies rather than tacking on extra rules to placate public unrest. This has worked well in South Korea, where nuclear power is operated by a subsidiary of KEPCO, a government corporation whose subsidiaries operate all of Korea’s electricity needs. Many of Korea’s reactors follow the standardized OPR-1000 and APR-1400 models, initiated and developed by a government program in the 1990s in collaboration with KEPCO. This close collaboration allowed the creation of a cheap, standardized reactor that meets safety standards, rather than forcing companies to meet arbitrary standards demanded by public opinion. 

Secondly, officials should reduce the extent to which perverse incentives can prevent policy change. Regulation is dominated by set-in-stone opinions, like the economic debates in 1970s America, and by the interests of external actors, like the Japanese “nuclear village” that demanded reopening after Fukushima. Unfortunately, resolving these issues is a tall order, and one that stretches well beyond the scope of nuclear energy policy. Gridlock and regulatory capture are well-known issues in the policy world that have countless proposed solutions. Since these solutions are highly dependent on governments’ unique political contexts, providing thorough recommendations is outside the scope of this paper.

While policy recommendations do not apply in every political context, understanding how fear-based nuclear energy policies are made makes it easier to avoid them in the future. Since fear of nuclear energy primarily comes from social amplification of risk, efforts to stop that amplification and provide true information can correct for biases in public opinion. Since political opportunities are necessary for public opinion and popular movements to affect policy, de-emphasizing the role of the public in nuclear regulation reduces the impact of unfounded fears. Finally, understanding the positive and negative feedback loops of the punctuated equilibrium model allows policymakers to target and eliminate the incentives that prevent regulation change. Changes to nuclear energy policy are desperately needed in the face of rising energy demand. Policymakers should use these models to implement those changes and bring back the dream of safe, clean, and limitless energy.
